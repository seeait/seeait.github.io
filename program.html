Keynote Session

Architects of Culture: Bridging Software Engineering and HR in the GenAI Era

by Dr. Magnus Lundbäck and Dr. Lucas Gren

Show abstract

GenAI adoption is moving fast—but culture often isn’t. This keynote discusses why AI transformation is today much more a people and change challenge than it is a technical one. Drawing from real-world case studies, this session will cover: i) how engineers and HR leaders can partner in the GenAI era, ii) why AI adoption should be treated as a sustainable change management process, and iii) how HR processes are evolving to support individuals and teams through this deep organizational change.

Dr. Magnus Lundbäck is the Executive Vice President of Human Resources @ Getinge, leading AI transformation efforts.

Dr. Lucas Gren is the Director of HR Process Automation and AI @ Getinge, and an active researcher at the Department of Computer Science and Engineering @ Chalmers | University of Gothenburg.

Accepted Papers

Generative AI and the Future of Professional Software Development: Survey Findings from Finland

Samuli Määttä (University of Oulu),
Markus Kelanti (University of Oulu),
Burak Turhan (University of Oulu)

Show abstract

In recent years, it has become common for generative AI-based software development tools to be experimented with, adopted, and used in professional software development. To understand the effects of this generative AI transformation in the Finnish context, we conducted a survey targeting software professionals working in Finland. 163 survey responses were collected during two months, from December 2025 to February 2026. In this paper, we report a qualitative analysis of 100 respondents’ perspectives on the future role of generative AI in professional software development, based on the responses to two optional open-ended questions in the survey. The results offer a practitioner-centred snapshot of how software professionals currently reason about the effects of generative AI on roles, skills, productivity, software quality, and the sustainability of expertise in the field.

Lifecycle-Aware GenAI Assistance with MCP via Context Refinement Loops: A Reference Architecture

Omar Elsisi (Toronto Metropolitan University),
Fabio De Abreu Santos (Colorado State University),
Glaucia Melo (Toronto Metropolitan University)

Show abstract

The rapid, often unstructured adoption of Generative AI (GenAI) in software-intensive organizations creates a socio-technical challenge. While GenAI can accelerate individual tasks, these gains are often offset by added coordination, verification, and governance effort across the software development lifecycle (SDLC). This paper presents DevCoach as a reference architecture built on the Model Context Protocol (MCP) to make GenAI assistance more lifecycle-aware, governable, and inspectable in organizational settings. DevCoach treats model outputs as proposals rather than authoritative actions, and couples generation to lifecycle-scoped retrieval, bounded tool use, deterministic validation, and provenance-linked audit artifacts. Its core mechanism is two Context Refinement Loops (CRLs): CRL 1 stabilizes user intent into an explicit retrieval contract, and CRL 2 performs bounded retrieval, evidence verification, and provenance construction to produce a verified context pack. The contribution is therefore architectural and design-oriented rather than a benchmarked implementation study. In this way, the paper reframes GenAI integration as a software engineering design and governance problem rather than a prompt-level optimization problem.

AI Safety Debt and Its Downstream Burden on Software Developers

Raymond Douglas (Alignment of Complex Systems Research Group),
Peter Wallich (Constellation Institute),
Gabriel Konar-Steenberg (London AI Safety Research Labs)

Show abstract

Software organisations building with generative AI face a problem that they did not create and cannot solve alone: the downstream burden of AI safety debt. AI safety debt — the gap between the safety approaches frontier labs use and those needed to justify the safety claims, implicit or explicit, expected from end users — produces consequences that fall on the downstream developer and the developer’s own users. Unlike traditional technical debt, this burden emanates from outside the organisation’s codebase, is partially beyond its control, and is growing as AI capabilities advance. First, we characterise three properties that make AI safety debt burdens harder to manage than familiar software risks: capabilities and contexts shift unpredictably, responsibilities and harms diffuse vertically, and adversaries and AI systems may actively exploit gaps. Second, we propose the AI safety debt burden register as a practical tracking tool and starting point for preparation.
